Dirichlet Process

** - under development - ** The Dirichlet process (DP) is a stochastic process whose realizations are themselves probability distributions. It is widely used in Bayesian nonparametrics when one does not want to fix in advance a finite-dimensional parametric family for the unknown distribution. Instead of assuming, for example, that the data arise from a normal distribution with unknown parameters, the Dirichlet process places a prior directly on the space of probability measures.

A Dirichlet process is written as

\[ G \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G_0), \]

where \(G_0\) is called the base distribution and \(\alpha > 0\) is called the concentration parameter.

The meaning of this notation is the following: for any measurable partition \(A_1,\dots,A_k\) of the sample space,

\[ \bigl(G(A_1),\dots,G(A_k)\bigr) \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}\bigl(\alpha G_0(A_1),\dots,\alpha G_0(A_k)\bigr). \]

This is the defining property of the Dirichlet process. It says that once the space is divided into finitely many disjoint regions, the random probabilities assigned by \(G\) to those regions follow a Dirichlet distribution.

Intuition

The Dirichlet process may be viewed as a probability distribution over probability distributions. Before observing the data, we do not commit to a single fixed distribution. Instead, we allow the unknown distribution itself to be random.

The base distribution \(G_0\) plays the role of a prior center. In fact,

\[ \mathbb{E}[G(A)] = G_0(A) \]

for every measurable set \(A\).

Thus, \(G_0\) represents the average shape of the random distribution \(G\). However, \(G\) will not usually be exactly equal to \(G_0\); it fluctuates around it.

The concentration parameter \(\alpha\) controls how strongly \(G\) is concentrated around \(G_0\):

  • if \(\alpha\) is large, then \(G\) tends to stay close to \(G_0\)
  • if \(\alpha\) is small, then \(G\) can deviate more substantially from \(G_0\)

So \(G_0\) determines the location of the prior belief, while \(\alpha\) determines the strength of that belief.

Finite-dimensional view

To understand the definition more concretely, suppose the sample space is partitioned into two sets, \(A\) and \(A^c\). Then

\[ \bigl(G(A), G(A^c)\bigr) \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}\bigl(\alpha G_0(A), \alpha G_0(A^c)\bigr). \]

Since the two components must sum to one, this reduces to a Beta distribution:

\[ G(A) \sim \mathrm{Beta}\bigl(\alpha G_0(A), \alpha(1-G_0(A))\bigr). \]

Hence, for every measurable set \(A\), the random mass \(G(A)\) is centered around \(G_0(A)\), with variability controlled by \(\alpha\).

Posterior conjugacy

One of the most attractive properties of the Dirichlet process is its conjugacy. Suppose

\[ X_1,\dots,X_n \mid G \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} G, \qquad G \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G_0). \]

Then the posterior distribution of \(G\) is also a Dirichlet process:

\[ G \mid X_1,\dots,X_n \sim \mathrm{DP}\left(\alpha+n,\; \frac{\alpha G_0 + \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_{X_i}}{\alpha+n}\right), \]

where \(\delta_{X_i}\) denotes a point mass at \(X_i\).

This expression has a very natural interpretation. The posterior base distribution is a weighted average of:

  • the prior base distribution \(G_0\)
  • the empirical distribution of the observed data

Thus, the Dirichlet process updates in a way that balances prior beliefs and observed evidence.

Why DP draws are discrete

A striking property of the Dirichlet process is that draws from it are almost surely discrete, even when the base distribution \(G_0\) is continuous. This is one of its most important theoretical features.

A constructive representation due to Sethuraman is the stick-breaking representation:

\[ G = \sum_{j=1}^\infty \pi_j \delta_{\theta_j}, \]

where

\[ \theta_j \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} G_0, \]

and the weights are given by

\[ \pi_j = V_j \prod_{\ell < j} (1-V_\ell), \qquad V_j \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} \mathrm{Beta}(1,\alpha). \]

This shows that a random draw from a Dirichlet process is an infinite discrete distribution made up of atoms \(\theta_j\) with random weights \(\pi_j\) that sum to one.

The term “stick-breaking” comes from the following intuition: start with a stick of length 1. Break off a random proportion \(V_1\) to form \(\pi_1\). Then break off a proportion \(V_2\) of what remains to form \(\pi_2\), and continue indefinitely.

Predictive interpretation: the Pólya urn view

Another important way to understand the Dirichlet process is through its predictive distribution. If

\[ X_1,\dots,X_n \mid G \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} G, \qquad G \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G_0), \]

then the predictive distribution for a new observation \(X_{n+1}\) is

\[ X_{n+1} \mid X_1,\dots,X_n \sim \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+n}G_0 + \frac{1}{\alpha+n}\sum_{i=1}^n \delta_{X_i}. \]

This formula is highly intuitive. The next draw is generated either:

  • from the base distribution \(G_0\), with probability proportional to \(\alpha\), or
  • by repeating one of the previously observed values, with probability proportional to how often it has already appeared

This clustering property is fundamental. It explains why the Dirichlet process is so useful for mixture modeling: observations tend to share atoms, which induces random clusters.

Dirichlet process mixtures

Since a draw from a Dirichlet process is discrete, a DP is not usually used directly as a prior for a smooth density. Instead, it is often used in a Dirichlet process mixture model.

A typical DP mixture model has the form

\[ X_i \mid \theta_i \sim F(\theta_i), \]

\[ \theta_i \mid G \sim G, \]

\[ G \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G_0). \]

Here, \(F(\theta_i)\) is a parametric kernel, such as a normal distribution with parameter \(\theta_i\). The latent parameters \(\theta_i\) are drawn from the random distribution \(G\), and because \(G\) is discrete, several \(\theta_i\) values may coincide. This induces a clustering structure among the observations.

The resulting marginal density may be written informally as

\[ f(x) = \int F(x \mid \theta)\, dG(\theta), \]

where \(G\) itself is random. In this way, the DP mixture model produces a flexible distributional model that can adapt to skewness, multimodality, and other deviations from simple parametric families.

Interpretation of the hyperparameters

The two hyperparameters of the Dirichlet process have distinct roles:

Base distribution \(G_0\)

The base distribution determines where the atoms are likely to be located. It is the centering distribution of the process and reflects prior beliefs about plausible parameter values or support points.

Concentration parameter \(\alpha\)

The concentration parameter controls how diffuse the random distribution is around \(G_0\), and also affects the clustering behavior:

  • small \(\alpha\) encourages fewer distinct clusters
  • large \(\alpha\) encourages more distinct clusters

Thus, \(\alpha\) controls the tendency to create new clusters, while \(G_0\) controls where those clusters are likely to appear.

Why the Dirichlet process is useful

The Dirichlet process is useful because it provides a principled Bayesian prior on unknown distributions without requiring a fixed finite-dimensional parametric form. It combines flexibility with tractability:

  • it is mathematically well defined
  • it has conjugate updating
  • it admits constructive representations
  • it naturally induces clustering
  • it is the foundation of many Bayesian nonparametric mixture models

For this reason, the Dirichlet process has become one of the central tools in Bayesian nonparametric statistics.

Summary

A Dirichlet process is a random probability measure \(G\) such that, for every finite measurable partition, the vector of probabilities assigned by \(G\) follows a Dirichlet distribution. It is denoted by

\[ G \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G_0), \]

where \(G_0\) is the base distribution and \(\alpha\) is the concentration parameter. The base distribution determines the prior center of the process, while the concentration parameter determines how strongly the random measure is concentrated around that center. Although draws from a Dirichlet process are almost surely discrete, the process is especially powerful when used in mixture models, where it provides a flexible and elegant prior for unknown distributions and latent cluster structures.

Stochastic Differential Equations and the Black–Scholes Model

1. Stochastic differential equations

A stochastic differential equation (SDE) is a differential equation that includes a random component. Ordinary differential equations describe how a variable changes over time in a fully deterministic way, whereas stochastic differential equations allow the evolution to be partly unpredictable.

A simple way to think about this is to imagine the price of a financial asset. Even if we believe that, on average, the price tends to grow over time, the actual path is never perfectly smooth. Instead, it moves up and down in a random way. An SDE is designed to capture both of these features at the same time: a systematic trend and random fluctuations.

A general SDE can be written as

\[ dX_t = \mu(X_t,t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t. \]

Here, \(X_t\) is the variable of interest at time \(t\), such as an asset price. The term \(\mu(X_t,t)\,dt\) is called the drift term and represents the predictable part of the motion. The term \(\sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t\) is called the diffusion term and represents the random part.

The process \(W_t\) is a Brownian motion or Wiener process. Intuitively, Brownian motion is a mathematical model for continuous random noise. Over a very short time interval \(dt\), the increment \(dW_t\) has mean zero and variance \(dt\). Thus,

\[ dW_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,dt) \]

in an informal sense.

This means that the drift term controls the average direction of motion, while the diffusion term controls the magnitude of the uncertainty. So an SDE can be read as follows:

the variable changes over a small time interval because of a systematic effect plus a random shock.

2. A basic financial example

In finance, one of the most important SDEs is the model for stock prices:

\[ dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t. \]

This says that the infinitesimal change in the stock price \(S_t\) has two parts:

  • a drift part, \(\mu S_t\,dt\), which represents the expected growth rate
  • a diffusion part, \(\sigma S_t\,dW_t\), which represents random market fluctuations

The factor \(S_t\) appears in both terms, meaning that changes are proportional to the current stock price. This is natural in finance, since a \(1\%\) move matters more for a stock priced at 100 than for one priced at 10.

This model is called geometric Brownian motion. It is the foundation of the Black–Scholes option pricing model.

3. Why geometric Brownian motion is useful

The SDE

\[ dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t \]

has several attractive features:

  1. it allows continuous random movement
  2. it ensures that stock prices remain positive
  3. it models returns rather than absolute price changes
  4. it leads to tractable mathematical formulas

Its explicit solution is

\[ S_t = S_0 \exp\left[\left(\mu-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t\right]. \]

This formula shows that \(\log S_t\) is normally distributed, so \(S_t\) itself is lognormally distributed.

This is an important idea in Black–Scholes: prices are modeled as positive random variables whose logarithms are Gaussian.

4. The intuition behind option pricing

An option is a financial contract whose value depends on an underlying asset. For example, a European call option gives its holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy a stock at a fixed price \(K\) at maturity \(T\).

Its payoff at maturity is

\[ (S_T-K)^+ = \max(S_T-K,0). \]

This means:

  • if \(S_T > K\), the option is worth \(S_T-K\)
  • if \(S_T \le K\), the option expires worthless

So the key problem is:

what is the fair value today of a payoff that depends on a random future stock price?

The Black–Scholes model answers this by combining the stock price SDE with a no-arbitrage argument.

5. The Black–Scholes stock price model

The Black–Scholes model assumes that the stock price follows geometric Brownian motion:

\[ dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t. \]

Here:

  • \(\mu\) is the expected return of the stock
  • \(\sigma\) is the volatility
  • \(W_t\) is Brownian motion

At first glance, it might seem that the option price should depend on \(\mu\), since \(\mu\) controls the stock’s expected growth. Surprisingly, in the Black–Scholes formula, the option price does not depend on \(\mu\).

The reason is that under the no-arbitrage principle, one can construct a hedged portfolio that eliminates risk over an infinitesimal interval. Once the risk is removed, the portfolio must grow at the risk-free rate \(r\). This leads to a pricing rule under the risk-neutral measure, where the stock effectively evolves as

\[ dS_t = r S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t. \]

So the expected return \(\mu\) is replaced by the risk-free rate \(r\) for pricing purposes.

6. The Black–Scholes partial differential equation

Let \(V(S,t)\) denote the price of an option as a function of the stock price \(S\) and time \(t\). By applying Itô’s lemma and constructing a riskless hedged portfolio, one obtains the Black–Scholes PDE:

\[ \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} +\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} +rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} -rV=0. \]

This equation says that the option price must evolve in a way consistent with no arbitrage.

Each term has an interpretation:

  • \(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}\) captures the effect of time passing
  • \(\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}\) captures the effect of uncertainty and curvature
  • \(rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\) reflects the financing effect from holding the underlying asset
  • \(-rV\) discounts the option value at the risk-free rate

So the Black–Scholes PDE is a balance equation ensuring that the option price is consistent with continuous hedging and no arbitrage.

7. The Black–Scholes formula for a European call option

Solving the PDE with terminal payoff

\[ V(S,T) = (S_T-K)^+ \]

gives the Black–Scholes formula for a European call:

\[ C(S_0,0)=S_0 N(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}N(d_2), \]

where

\[ d_1=\frac{\ln(S_0/K)+(r+\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt{T}. \]

Here, \(N(\cdot)\) is the standard normal cumulative distribution function.

Similarly, the European put price is

\[ P(S_0,0)=Ke^{-rT}N(-d_2)-S_0N(-d_1). \]

8. Intuition for the Black–Scholes formula

The call price

\[ C=S_0N(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}N(d_2) \]

can be understood intuitively as:

  • the current stock price, adjusted for the probability of finishing in the money
  • minus the present value of the strike price, also adjusted for that probability

The term \(N(d_2)\) is often interpreted as the risk-neutral probability that the option finishes in the money, while \(N(d_1)\) is related to the hedge ratio, or delta, of the option.

So the Black–Scholes formula is not just a mysterious closed form. It is the result of:

  1. modeling the stock with an SDE
  2. eliminating risk by hedging
  3. using no-arbitrage pricing
  4. solving the resulting differential equation

9. Why volatility matters so much

In the Black–Scholes model, volatility \(\sigma\) plays a central role. Higher volatility increases the uncertainty of the stock price at maturity. For a call option, this is valuable because the payoff is asymmetric:

  • downside is limited to zero
  • upside is unbounded

Therefore, greater volatility generally increases the value of both calls and puts.

This is one reason option prices are highly sensitive to volatility, and why implied volatility is such an important concept in practice.

10. Summary intuition

A stochastic differential equation models a system that evolves with both predictable and random components. In finance, the Black–Scholes model assumes that stock prices follow the SDE

\[ dS_t=\mu S_t\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t. \]

Under no-arbitrage pricing, the drift is replaced by the risk-free rate, leading to the Black–Scholes pricing equation and ultimately to the closed-form option pricing formula. Intuitively, the model says that stock prices move continuously but randomly, and the value of an option is the present value of its future payoff under this risk-adjusted probabilistic framework.
